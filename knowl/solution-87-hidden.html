To find candidates for inflection points, we solve \(P''(t)=0\)
\begin{align*} P''(t) \amp = 0\\ -.6t + 3.6 \amp = 0 \quad \textrm{(multiply both sides by 10)} \\ -6t + 36\amp = 0 \\ -6(t-6) \amp = 0 \end{align*}
and we see that \(t=6\) may give an inflection point. Let's make a sign chart for the second derivative.
  • In the interval \([2, 6)\) we can test with \(t = 3\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} P''(3) = -6(3-6) > 0 \end{equation*}
  • In the interval \((6,10]\) we can test with \(t = 7\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} P''(7) = -6(7-6) < 0 \end{equation*}
The sign chart for \(P''(t)\) is below. Notice that the concavity of \(P\) is labeled below the intervals.
Since the sign of \(P''\) changes about \(t=6\text{,}\) we can conclude that the point \((6,P(6)) = (6,19.6)\) is an inflection point. Comparing the sign charts for \(P'\) and \(P''\) we see that
  • \(P\) is deceasing at an increasing rate from \(t=2\) to \(t=3\)
  • \(P\) is increasing at an increasing rate from \(t=3\) to \(t=6\)
  • \(P\) is increasing at a decreasing rate from \(t=6\) to \(t=9\)
  • \(P\) is decreasing at a decreasing rate from \(t=9\) to \(t=10\)
From the second and third bullet points above, we can now conclude that (6,19.6) is a point of diminishing returns. That is, Pendant Publusihng had a point of diminishing returns in 2004 during which year their profit was $19,600,000.
