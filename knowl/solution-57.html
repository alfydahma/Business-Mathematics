Solution a.1.

Let's first simplify the difference quotient. Most of the time, that just means simplify until you can cancel the \(h\) in the denominator:
\begin{align*} \dfrac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} \amp = \dfrac{2(x+h)^2 - 3(x+h) -1 - (2x^2 - 3x -1)}{h} \\ \amp = \dfrac{2x^2 + 4xh + 2h^2 - 3x - 3h -1 -2x^2 +3x +1}{h} \\ \amp = \dfrac{h(4x+2h-3)}{h} = 4x + 2h - 3 \end{align*}
Now, when we take the limit as \(h\) goes to 0, we can just substitute 0 in for \(h\) (since we can view the simplified difference quotient as a polynomial in \(h\)). So,
\begin{align*} f'(x) = \dfrac{df}{dx} \amp = \lim_{h \rightarrow 0}\dfrac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} = \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} (4x + 2h-3) \\ \amp = 4x + 2 \cdot 0 -3 = 4x - 3. \end{align*}
That is, the derivative of \(f(x)=2x^2 -3x -1\) is \(f'(x) = \dfrac{df}{dx} = 4x - 3 \text{.}\)
